Where Is the File?

Medium Version

After decades of working in tech and teaching programming, I’ve realized something surprising:

There’s really only one skill you need to feel comfortable with technology—and it’s actually a question:

“Where is the file?”

Every time you save something, your computer doesn’t just tuck it neatly away. It turns your file into bits, sends those bits across wires or Wi-Fi, often across a WAN (wide area network), and stores them on physical machines—sometimes across the country or the world.

And not just once.

It may create multiple copies:

  • a local version on your device
  • a synced version in the cloud
  • backups on other servers
  • cached versions for speed

So the real skill isn’t “knowing apps” or “being good with computers.”

It’s knowing:

  • where your data currently lives
  • where it will be stored next
  • how many copies exist

Because once copies multiply without your awareness, control disappears.

The goal is simple: one file—and you know exactly where it is.

Happy computing.

Where Is the File?

Long Version

After decades of working in technology—and teaching students how to program—I’ve come to a conclusion that surprises people:

There is really only one skill you need to feel comfortable with technology.

And it’s not coding.
It’s not troubleshooting.
It’s not memorizing tools.

It’s a single question:

“Where is the file?”

Because everything in computing—every app, every system, every “save” button—comes down to the movement and storage of data.

When you create or save a file, here’s what’s actually happening:

Your file is converted into binary data—bits. Those bits don’t just sit in one place. They move.

If you’re saving locally, they’re written to a storage device—an SSD or hard drive—on your machine.

If you’re saving to the cloud, something far more complex happens:

  • Your file is broken into packets
  • Those packets are sent across your local network
  • Then across a WAN—possibly across cities, states, or continents
  • They are reassembled on a remote server
  • That server may immediately replicate your file across multiple data centers
  • Additional systems may cache, index, or back it up

Within seconds, your “one file” may exist in several places:

  • your device
  • a cloud server
  • a backup server
  • a redundancy system
  • maybe even a temporary cache somewhere in between

And yet, from your perspective, it still looks like a single file.

This illusion is where confusion—and risk—begins.

Because once you lose track of:

  • where the file actually lives
  • which version is the “real” one
  • how many copies exist

you lose control.

You overwrite the wrong version.
You delete one copy, thinking it’s the only one.
Or worse—you assume something is saved when it isn’t.

So the true skill in technology is awareness.

Not surface-level usage—but spatial and conceptual awareness of your data:

  • Where is it right now?
  • Where is it going next?
  • How many versions or copies exist?
  • Which one is the source of truth?

If you can answer those questions confidently, you are no longer guessing—you are operating with intent.

And that leads to a simple philosophy:

Your goal: Keep it to one file—and know exactly where it is.

Everything else—apps, platforms, systems—is just a layer on top of that truth.

Happy computing.
